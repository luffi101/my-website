Name: Jane Austen
Date of Birth - Date of Death: 16 December 1775 – 18 July 1817
Nationality: English
Region of the world: Europe
Description of his significance:
Austen’s novels, including Pride and Prejudice and Sense and Sensibility, offered keen social commentary on 19th-century English society. She is celebrated for her sharp wit, incisive observation of manners, and pioneering role in the development of the modern novel.

Name: Victor Hugo
Date of Birth - Date of Death: 26 February 1802 – 22 May 1885
Nationality: French
Region of the world: Europe
Description of his significance:
Hugo was a prominent figure in the Romantic movement, best known for his novels Les Misérables and The Hunchback of Notre-Dame. He was instrumental in shaping French literature and politics, advocating social justice and championing the poor and oppressed.

---
Name: Edgar Allan Poe
Date of Birth - Date of Death: 19 January 1809 – 7 October 1849
Nationality: American
Region of the world: North America
Description of his significance:
Poe pioneered the modern short story and detective fiction genres, and his poetry and tales of mystery and the macabre have had a lasting influence on literature. His use of gothic themes and psychological depth helped define American Romanticism.

---
Name: Charles Dickens
Date of Birth - Date of Death: 7 February 1812 – 9 June 1870
Nationality: English
Region of the world: Europe
Description of his significance:
Dickens is regarded as one of the greatest novelists of the Victorian era, crafting enduring works such as A Christmas Carol, Oliver Twist, and Great Expectations. His novels brought attention to social and economic injustices, influencing reforms in 19th-century England.

---
Name: Charlotte Brontë
Date of Birth - Date of Death: 21 April 1816 – 31 March 1855
Nationality: English
Region of the world: Europe
Description of his significance:
Known primarily for her novel Jane Eyre, Charlotte Brontë explored complex emotional themes and societal norms. Her strong, introspective heroines and intimate narrative style contributed significantly to the evolution of the modern novel.

---
Name: Emily Brontë
Date of Birth - Date of Death: 30 July 1818 – 19 December 1848
Nationality: English
Region of the world: Europe
Description of his significance:
Author of Wuthering Heights, Emily Brontë’s work is celebrated for its intense passion and innovative narrative structure. Though she published only one novel, it remains a cornerstone of English literature and the Romantic movement.

---
Name: Herman Melville
Date of Birth - Date of Death: 1 August 1819 – 28 September 1891
Nationality: American
Region of the world: North America
Description of his significance:
Melville’s Moby-Dick stands as a monumental work of American literature. His adventurous life at sea informed his storytelling, which combined philosophical depth, narrative experimentation, and a critique of 19th-century American society.

---
Name: Fyodor Dostoevsky
Date of Birth - Date of Death: 11 November 1821 – 9 February 1881
Nationality: Russian
Region of the world: Europe
Description of his significance:
Dostoevsky’s novels, including Crime and Punishment and The Brothers Karamazov, delved deeply into human psychology, morality, and social issues. His exploration of existential themes profoundly influenced literary modernism and existential philosophy.

---
Name: Leo Tolstoy
Date of Birth - Date of Death: 9 September 1828 – 20 November 1910
Nationality: Russian
Region of the world: Europe
Description of his significance:
Tolstoy’s epic novels War and Peace and Anna Karenina are celebrated for their sweeping scope, detailed realism, and philosophical reflections on human existence. A leading figure in realist literature, Tolstoy also became a moral and spiritual thinker later in life.

---
Name: Mark Twain (Samuel Langhorne Clemens)
Date of Birth - Date of Death: 30 November 1835 – 21 April 1910
Nationality: American
Region of the world: North America
Description of his significance:
Twain’s works, notably The Adventures of Tom Sawyer and Adventures of Huckleberry Finn, captured American life along the Mississippi River and critiqued social norms with humor and satire. He is often called the “father of American literature” for his distinctive voice and influence.
